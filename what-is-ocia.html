New to the Faith

What is OCIA?

The ordinary parish route for adults who want to enquire about becoming Catholic, or about completing their Christian initiation.

Home / New to the faith / What is OCIA?

OCIA stands for the Order of Christian Initiation of Adults. In most parishes it is the ordinary way adults are introduced to Catholic life when they are enquiring seriously about becoming Catholic, being baptised, being confirmed, or being received more fully into the Church.

It is best understood as a parish journey rather than a content download. It normally includes conversation, prayer, learning, Scripture, reflection, contact with parish life, and a gradual sense of what Catholic worship and belonging actually involve.

No need to rush

  • Asking about OCIA does not force an immediate decision.
  • Many people attend enquiry meetings before deciding whether to continue.
  • Parishes usually expect questions, uncertainty, and a gradual pace.
What it is

A period of learning, prayer, and reflection

OCIA includes teaching, but it is not merely an information-transfer course. It is meant to help a person begin to know the faith from within: through the Gospel, prayer, parish life, and reflection on whether this is the life to which God may be calling them.

What it is not

Not merely an online course or a box to tick

OCIA is not simply a set of videos to complete or facts to memorise. It is also not a social performance. The aim is not speed, but honest preparation and real contact with Catholic life.

Typical shape

How it usually unfolds

Parishes differ, but the shape is often similar: an initial enquiry, a period of learning and discussion, growing participation in parish life, and then more formal preparation for reception into the Church if that is freely chosen.

The practical details vary widely. Some parishes use weekly meetings, some combine talks and discussion, and some share resources for reading between meetings.

Typical timing

When it usually happens

Enquiry can begin at any time. More structured preparation often runs from autumn to Easter, and reception into the Church often takes place at Easter, but the pace can vary according to the parish and the person's readiness.

Different starting points

If you have already been baptised

Not everyone arrives in the same position. Some people have never been baptised. Some were baptised in another Christian community. Some were baptised Catholic but were never confirmed or never received much formation.

A priest or parish team can help identify what applies in your case. That is one reason not to assume your route must look exactly like someone else's.

A first conversation

What to say when you enquire

If you want to make contact, keep it simple: "I am exploring the Catholic faith and would like to ask about OCIA or an enquiry conversation." That is enough.

A good parish will understand that people often begin quietly, with real questions and without a fixed timetable.
