Retreat

Retreats in Britain & Ireland

Learn what a Catholic retreat is, why people make one, and where you can find quiet houses, spiritual centres, and simpler retreat options close to home.

What is a retreat?

Stepping back so prayer can breathe

A retreat is not mainly a holiday and it is not a test of spiritual strength. It is time set aside for God: time to pray, listen, rest, examine your life honestly, and let the noise of ordinary routines quieten down for a while.

Sometimes that means silence in a retreat house. Sometimes it means a guided day, a parish quiet day in Lent, a weekend with conferences and Mass, or a simple day of recollection made at home. The point is not performance. The point is attention, honesty, and room for grace.

When and why

When people usually make retreats

  • In Advent or Lent, when the Church already invites a more recollected pace.
  • At moments of grief, fatigue, decision, or spiritual dryness.
  • Before a big step in life: marriage, vocation, a move, retirement, or return to the sacraments.
  • Simply because prayer has become thin and needs time, quiet, and renewal.

Common forms

  • Day retreats and quiet days.
  • Weekend retreats.
  • Individually guided retreats.
  • Silent retreats.
  • Parish group retreats.
  • Retreats at home or online.

Closer to home

Retreats in parish, in daily life, and at home

Not everyone can disappear for a week. That does not mean retreat is closed to them. Some of the best first steps are small and local: a parish quiet morning, a diocesan day retreat, a monthly day of recollection, a guided online retreat, or a day of silence built around Mass and prayer.

Simple pattern

How to make a small retreat in ordinary life

  • Choose a half-day or full day and protect it from errands and messages.
  • Begin with Mass if possible, or with a Psalm and a slow reading from a Gospel.
  • Keep an hour of silence, with no music, social media, or news.
  • Take a walk and pray the Rosary, the Angelus, or the Divine Mercy Chaplet.
  • Spend time with one piece of spiritual reading rather than skimming many things.
  • End with a brief examen: gratitude, sorrow, grace received, and what God may be asking next.

Good parish options

  • Lent or Advent mornings of recollection.
  • Men's or women's days of prayer.
  • First Saturday Marian mornings.
  • Holy Hour plus conference and confessions.
  • Parish missions or evenings with a visiting priest or religious.
Keep looking

Begin locally if you need to

If you are not ready to book a residential retreat, begin with your parish or diocese. A priest, parish office, or diocesan events page will often know about quiet days, confession evenings, diocesan retreats, and places nearby that do not always appear in general searches.
