AN ORDINARY CATHOLIC LEARNING DELIBERATELY

The Catholic Experiment

Can an ordinary working Catholic build a serious, structured education in the faith without becoming permanently attached to a screen?

I am Alan Gallagher, a Catholic convert and the learner behind this experiment. I am using books, notebooks, memory, authoritative Catholic sources and my background in technology to organise, test and document a long-term course of study.

Why I began

I attended Mass for around ten years before being received into the Church. During that time, and in the years since, I read books, watched videos, followed Catholic websites and gradually learned a great deal about the faith. But most of that learning was passive. I rarely documented what I had studied, tested what I remembered or followed a clear path from one subject to the next.

The Catholic Experiment began when I decided not simply to consume more information, but to return to the faith deliberately and study it in depth.

I already owned many Catholic books. As I began exploring how I might use them more seriously, I also discovered just how rich the modern research environment has become. Historic books and courses can now sit alongside digital libraries, Church documents, the Catechism, papal teaching, lectures and other resources that are often freely available.

The difficulty is no longer simply finding information. It is deciding what to study, in what order, how to judge it and how to retain what has been learned.

I needed structure. I also work full time and did not want Catholic study to mean spending every remaining hour staring at another screen. So I began looking at older and more active methods of learning: handwriting, copying important passages, reading aloud, repetition, recall, practical exercises and keeping a physical notebook.

I was particularly inspired by the disciplined learning associated with monastic life: texts read slowly, prayers and psalms learned through repetition, and knowledge developed through patient daily practice rather than endless browsing.

I also have a background in technology. I decided to put those skills to use—not to replace learning, but to organise the material, build a clear route through it, document progress and make the results accessible to others.

How the Experiment works

Each Experiment begins with a serious book, course or collection of primary material. That older source provides the spine rather than being replaced by a website.

I then work through it actively:

  • reading slowly and deliberately;
  • writing by hand;
  • copying and defining key terms;
  • completing exercises;
  • reading aloud where useful;
  • testing recall;
  • recording questions and uncertainties;
  • checking older material against authoritative modern Catholic sources;
  • reflecting honestly on what worked and what did not.

The point is not to produce the appearance of expertise. The point is to find out whether I genuinely learn, retain and can use what I have studied.

Long-term plan

The Schools

The faith is too broad to treat as one undifferentiated course. The Schools organise the long-term journey without pretending every course already exists.

School of Faith

Catholic doctrine, catechesis, prayer, sacramental life and the practical living of the faith.

School of Sacred Scripture

The Bible, its structure, interpretation, history and place within the life of the Church.

School of Language

Latin and other languages that provide access to Catholic texts, worship and intellectual tradition.

School of Reason

Logic, philosophy, natural theology, argument and the relationship between faith and reason.

School of History

The history of the Church, councils, saints, institutions, controversies and the wider world in which Catholic life developed.

Follow the Experiment

I intend to document the work honestly: what I study, how long it takes, what I retain, which methods help, where older sources need explanation and where the structure becomes unrealistic.

Some of that record will appear on this website. I also intend to make occasional videos documenting progress and showing the notebooks, materials and practical methods involved.

Others are welcome to follow the same courses, adapt the methods to their own circumstances, suggest useful sources and point out mistakes. This is a personal educational project, not an official teaching authority. Personal interpretation will be identified as such, and corrections are welcome.

The Church has accumulated nearly two thousand years of prayer, thought, argument, art, scholarship and lived experience. Much of it is now more accessible than at any previous point in history. The Catholic Experiment asks what might happen when an ordinary working Catholic stops browsing it passively and begins to study it deliberately.

Materials

The book remains central. The aim is to make serious study possible with simple physical materials and lawful access to the relevant sources.

Practical

What you need to study

Keep the essential kit modest. Anything beyond the required items is optional.

Required

  • One ruled notebook or exercise book
  • Pen
  • Pencil and eraser
  • Access to the course text

Recommended

  • Ruler for clear page layout
  • Dictionary appropriate to the subject
  • Headphones for listening work

Optional

  • Flashcards
  • Separate vocabulary notebook
  • Printer or loose-leaf folder
